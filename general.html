(3) Neuromorphic Devices and Systems:

    • Speaker
      Prof. Tobi Delbruck
    • Institution
      ETH Zurich, Switzerland
    • Email
      tobi@ini.uzh.ch
    • Website
      click here
    • Description
      Prof. Delbruck is a pioneer in developing neuromorphic vision sensors for real-world applications in machine vision. He has designed chips for automatic focusing, imagers that compute temporal derivatives of the image, visual motion, and stereo matching. He built the first analog VLSI autofocusing chip based on biological accommodation principles, one of the first silicon retinas with a full set of velocity tuned pixels, and together with Misha Mahowald, the first analog VLSI cooperative stereo matching circuit. With Carver Mead, he jointly invented a widely used adaptive photoreceptor circuit. He invented the bump circuit, which is commonly used to measure similarity or dissimilarity of signals.

(4) Neuromorphic Computation with Devices in Advanced Technologies:

    • Speaker
      Prof. Giacomo Indiveri
    • Institution
      ETH Zurich, Switzerland
    • Email
      giacomo@ini.phys.ethz.ch
    • Website
      click here
    • Description
      Prof. Indiveri is a multi-faceted scholar in studying both real and artificial neural processing systems, and by building hardware neuromorphic cognitive systems, using full custom analog and digital VLSI technology. He develops computational models, hybrid analog/digital VLSI circuits, and multi-chip event-based systems for implementing neuromorphic cognitive systems. His work is aimed at bridging the gap from simple reactive neuromorphic sensory-motor systems to ones that are cognitive in quality: e.g. neuromorphic architectures that can reason about the actions to take, in response to the combinations of external stimuli, internal states, and behavioral objectives. His contributions to the neuromorphic VLSI technology consist of novel low-power silicon neuron circuits, dynamic silicon synapses, hybrid analog/digital spike-based learning mechanisms, soft Winner-take-all networks, and asynchronous digital communication circuits and systems.
